# Synthetic Example Discovery Call - ACME Inc.

## Call Metadata

- Date: 2026-04-21
- Call type: Discovery call
- Company: ACME Inc.
- Prospect: Priya Nair, Chief of Staff
- Seller: Alex Morgan, Account Executive
- Product context: A knowledge base product designed to help organisations compound customer learning, decisions, operating context, and playbooks over time
- Synthetic: Yes

## Transcript

**Alex:** Priya, thanks for making the time. Before we jump in, would it be helpful if I propose a simple agenda? I was thinking: first I understand what prompted you to look at this now, then how you're currently managing knowledge across the business, where that breaks down, and if it makes sense we can talk about what a useful next step would be. Does that work?

**Priya:** Yes, that's perfect.

**Alex:** Great. To start there, what prompted you to take this conversation now?

**Priya:** We're scaling fast, and I think we've hit the point where context is leaking everywhere. Customer calls happen, product decisions get made, operating lessons pile up, but six weeks later nobody can trace why something happened or what we learned. We have a lot of information, but not much shared understanding.

**Alex:** When you say context is leaking, where do you see that most clearly today?

**Priya:** Three places. First, customer insight. Interviews sit in call recordings, scattered notes, or somebody's private docs. Second, decision-making. A lot of key product and operating decisions happen in Slack or meetings and then never get captured in a durable way. Third, onboarding. New leaders spend an absurd amount of time reconstructing what the company already knows.

**Alex:** That's helpful. Can you walk me through how you're managing that today, even if it's messy?

**Priya:** Sure. Customer research lives partly in our call-recording tool, partly in Notion, partly in Google Docs. Product specs live in Linear docs and Notion. Strategy is usually in decks. Decisions are in meeting notes if we're lucky. Team rituals are in a wiki that nobody fully trusts. So technically the information exists, but it doesn't connect.

**Alex:** What parts of that current setup are working reasonably well?

**Priya:** Individual teams can usually find their own stuff. Research can find research. Product can find product docs. RevOps can find enablement material. The problem starts when you need to answer a cross-functional question like, "What are the top customer pain points from the last month, and how does that compare to what we're building now?" Then everyone assembles a different answer.

**Alex:** That's a strong example. Can you give me a recent instance where that happened?

**Priya:** We had a roadmap review two weeks ago. One PM said our biggest issue in mid-market was reporting depth. Our research lead said the recent interviews pointed more to handoff reliability and decision traceability. Then sales said the top objection was actually implementation overhead. All three had evidence, but it lived in different places and wasn't synthesized in the same way. We spent half the meeting debating whose picture of reality was current.

**Alex:** Of the issues you've mentioned, which three or four feel most acute for you right now?

**Priya:** Number one is fragmented customer learning. Number two is weak decision traceability. Number three is duplicated work because teams keep rediscovering things. And number four is slow onboarding for anyone stepping into a leadership or cross-functional role.

**Alex:** I'd love to go one level deeper on each of those. Starting with fragmented customer learning, what is the actual cost of that for ACME?

**Priya:** Product and go-to-market are often operating off slightly different narratives. Research might hear one pattern, sales hears another, and product prioritizes based on a third interpretation. That creates thrash. We revisit decisions, and we lose confidence that we're building against the sharpest understanding of the customer.

**Alex:** And on decision traceability, what tends to happen when that breaks down?

**Priya:** We repeat old debates. Someone asks why we chose not to go after a segment, or why we designed a workflow in a certain way, and nobody can find the reasoning. The people who were in the room may remember it, but the organisation doesn't. So context becomes personality-dependent.

**Alex:** How about duplicated work?

**Priya:** Teams rerun analysis that already exists. Customer success writes a summary, product research writes a separate summary, and strategy writes yet another. We pay the tax multiple times because there isn't a trusted path from evidence to insight to something the broader company can actually use.

**Alex:** That phrase is useful: a trusted path from evidence to insight to something the company can use. When onboarding is slow, what does that look like in practice?

**Priya:** New leaders shadow people, read old docs, ask around in Slack, and still don't know what's canonical. They can collect information, but they don't inherit understanding. It probably adds a month or two before they can make high-quality decisions independently.

**Alex:** If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what happens?

**Priya:** We get noisier as we scale. More tools, more summaries, more AI-generated artifacts, but not necessarily more clarity. I think we'd continue to ship things, but with more rework, weaker alignment, and slower learning loops. The organisation would look productive without really compounding.

**Alex:** Have you already tried to solve this? If so, what have you tried, and what happened?

**Priya:** We tried tightening documentation standards, but compliance faded after a quarter. We tried creating a central wiki, but it became a dumping ground. We experimented with AI summaries from customer calls, which helped with speed but not with synthesis or long-term retrieval. The missing piece is not just storage. It's structure, traceability, and a model for turning raw material into shared knowledge.

**Alex:** If this worked exactly the way you hoped, what would be different six months from now?

**Priya:** I'd want a few things to be true. One, recent customer evidence could be turned into clear, queryable insights. Two, strategy and product decisions would explicitly link back to those insights. Three, teams could answer important questions without pulling a scavenger hunt across five tools. And four, onboarding into company context would be much faster.

**Alex:** When you think about evaluating a solution here, what matters most?

**Priya:** First, it has to preserve traceability from evidence to insight to knowledge. Second, it has to be lightweight enough that teams will actually use it. Third, it needs to work across functions, not just for research or product. And fourth, it can't pretend to replace systems of record like CRM or project tools. We want a knowledge engine, not another place to duplicate operational state.

**Alex:** That's very aligned with how we think about it. Who else would need to be involved if this moved forward?

**Priya:** Our VP of Product, our Research Lead, and likely someone from RevOps because customer knowledge doesn't only live in product research for us. If we liked what we saw, IT would want a view as well, mostly around access and governance.

**Alex:** And is there a timeline or forcing event behind this, or is it more exploratory right now?

**Priya:** There is a forcing event. We're heading into second-half planning in about seven weeks. I'd like a better system in place, or at least a clear path, before that cycle starts. Otherwise we're going to run another planning round with fragmented inputs.

**Alex:** That makes sense. Before I suggest a next step, let me reflect back what I heard and you can tell me if I missed anything. You're not trying to solve for more raw information. You're trying to create a trusted path from customer evidence to synthesized insight to durable company knowledge. The biggest pain points are fragmented customer learning, weak decision traceability, duplicated analysis, and slow onboarding. And the urgency is that second-half planning is coming up and you want a stronger shared context before then. Is that a fair summary?

**Priya:** That's exactly right.

**Alex:** Great. Based on that, I don't think the right next step is a generic demo. I'd suggest a working session with you, product, and research where we use a sanitized example from your world and show how the flow would work: evidence in, insights synthesized, and then linked strategy, decisions, and playbooks on top. Would that be useful?

**Priya:** Yes, that would be much more useful than a feature tour.

**Alex:** Perfect. For that session, what would you want to walk away with?

**Priya:** I want to see whether your model can answer a question our leadership team actually asks, like: what are the top customer pain points from the past 30 days, and how do they compare to what we're building? If your workflow can make that answer faster and more reliable, that would be compelling.

**Alex:** That's a strong use case. I'll send a proposed agenda for a 60-minute working session and a short list of the materials we'd want you to bring. Does next Tuesday or Wednesday afternoon work better?

**Priya:** Wednesday afternoon is better.

**Alex:** Great. I'll follow up with times. Priya, thanks again. This was really clear.

**Priya:** Thank you. Looking forward to the next conversation.

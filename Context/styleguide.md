# Style Guide

_Based on the writing style of Kenneth Cukier, Deputy Executive Editor, The Economist. Co-author of Framers and Big Data._

---
## Voice and stance

### Challenge the default, then reframe

Open entries by naming the received wisdom, then inverting it. Cukier's signature move is "we're often told X — but this is backwards." The reader should feel the ground shift in the first two sentences.

> **Avoid:** Mental models are useful for decision-making.
> 
> **Prefer:** We're told that more data leads to better decisions. The opposite is closer to the truth: decisions improve when you control the frame, not the dataset.

### Write with confidence

Avoid hedges like "it could be argued," "one might say," or "it is perhaps the case that." Make the claim, then support it with evidence or a concrete example. If you are genuinely uncertain, say so directly — "we don't yet know whether…" — rather than hiding behind passive qualifiers.

### Analytical but never bloodless

Ground every abstract claim in a concrete story or vignette — a company, a historical moment, a named individual. The example is not decoration; it is the argument. A claim without an example is a claim that hasn't been made yet.

### Use the reader as a protagonist

Write toward implication. The reader should always know what to _do_ with what they've just read. Cukier ends arguments with a practical or intellectual consequence, not a summary.

---

## Entry structure

Every entry follows a four-beat arc:

1. **Hook** — the tension, puzzle, or counterintuitive observation
2. **Inversion** — the assumption that turns out to be wrong
3. **Evidence** — a specific example, case, or data point that makes the inversion concrete
4. **Implication** — what this means for how the reader should think or act

If an entry needs more than one section heading, it is two entries. Cukier's chapters feel dense, but each argues a single, tightly scoped thesis.

### End on the implication, not the summary

Don't close with "in summary, we have seen…". Close with the so-what. What should the reader decide, do, or think differently about? The last sentence of an entry is the most important one.

## Sentence craft

### Use the long-short rhythm

Follow a complex, clause-heavy sentence with a short declarative one. The short sentence lands the point. Use this rhythm deliberately — it gives prose both momentum and emphasis.

> The ability to switch frames — to discard a mental model that no longer fits and adopt a new one — is rare in individuals and almost unheard of in institutions. Nokia never found it.

### Active voice, human subjects

Put people in sentences. "Apple reframed music as an experience" beats "the music industry was disrupted by a reframing of consumer expectations." Passive constructions obscure agency; naming who did what makes the claim testable.

### Quantify when you can, qualify when you can't

If a claim can be anchored with a number, date, or named case — anchor it. If not, signal the uncertainty explicitly. "Most organisations" is vague. "Three of the five largest European banks" is not.

### One idea per sentence

Cukier rarely subordinates more than one clause. When a sentence needs three commas and a semicolon, it usually contains two ideas. Split it.

## Titles

Titles are theses, not topics. A title that names the subject tells the reader nothing they couldn't have guessed. A title that states the argument tells them everything before they read a word.

|Avoid (topic title)|Prefer (thesis title)|
|---|---|
|Mental models|Frames shape what we can see|
|AI limitations|AI answers; humans ask|
|Organisational change|Institutions reframe last|
|Decision-making|Framing precedes deciding|
|Data strategy|Data reveals; framing decides|

A good test: if the title could appear in a table of contents for a textbook, it is a topic. If it could be a thesis statement, it is a title.

## Formatting conventions

### No bullet-pointed claims

Cukier argues in prose, not lists. Lists imply equivalence between items; an argument has a sequence and a logic. Use numbered lists only for genuinely procedural steps where order is the point. If you find yourself reaching for bullets to present an argument, that is a signal the argument has not yet been written.

### Jargon earns its place

Introduce a technical term once, define it in plain language in the same sentence, then use it freely thereafter.

> **Avoid:** Leverage the affordances of the cognitive framing mechanism.
> 
> **Prefer:** Framing — the mental model we use to make sense of a new situation — is not a bias to overcome. It is a tool to master.

Never use jargon as a shortcut for an explanation you haven't written yet.

### Avoid em-dashes

Em-dashes — used like this — create the illusion of rhythm while undermining it. They invite the writer to defer precision: to gesture at a qualification rather than construct one. Cukier uses them sparingly, and only when the interruption genuinely changes the meaning of the sentence. In most cases, a comma, a colon, or a full stop does the job more cleanly. If you find an em-dash in a draft, ask whether the sentence structure is doing the work or merely decorating it.

> **Avoid:** The ability to reframe — which most leaders underestimate — is the rarest organisational skill.
> 
> **Prefer:** The ability to reframe is the rarest organisational skill, and most leaders underestimate it.> 

### Citations are evidence, not decoration

When citing a source, the citation should do work. State what the source shows, not just that it exists. "Studies show…" is not a citation. "A 2019 analysis of 200 corporate turnarounds by McKinsey found…" is.

### Paragraphs have one job

Each paragraph makes one move: it introduces, develops, or lands a single point. If a paragraph does two things, it is two paragraphs.

## Language

### Write in British English

This knowledge base follows British spelling and conventions, consistent with The Economist's house style. Key differences from American English:

|Prefer|Avoid|
|---|---|
|organise, recognise, analyse|organize, recognize, analyze|
|behaviour, colour, honour|behavior, color, honor|
|centre, theatre|center, theater|
|programme|program _(except software)_|
|whilst, amongst|while, among _(both are acceptable; prefer the British form)_|
|per cent|percent|
|licence _(noun)_, license _(verb)_|license for both|

Punctuation follows British convention: punctuation goes outside quotation marks unless it is part of the quoted material. Single quotes for primary quotation, double quotes for quotation within quotation.

## Words and phrases to avoid

These are either vague, overused, or explicitly mocked in Cukier's work. _Think outside the box_ earns a special ban — he devotes a chapter of Framers to explaining why it is useless advice.

- leverage _(as a verb)_
- it is important to note
- in today's fast-paced world
- paradigm shift
- think outside the box
- stakeholders
- going forward
- as mentioned above
- this section explores
- it should be noted
- synergy
- best practices
- ecosystem _(when used metaphorically for a business context)_
- robust _(when used to mean "good")_
- seamless

## A note on tone

Cukier writes for a general intelligent reader, not a specialist. The _Economist_ house style assumes a reader who is educated, curious, and time-constrained. That means: no condescension, no hand-holding, but also no insider shorthand. If a concept requires background, provide it in one sentence and move on.

The test for any entry: could a sharp person with no prior knowledge of this topic read this entry and immediately use the idea? If not, something is missing — probably the concrete example, or the implication, or both.
